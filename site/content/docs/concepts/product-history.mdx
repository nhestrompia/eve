---
title: Product History
description: What product history means and how eve records it.
---

Product history is the explanation of what changed in the product and why the change can be trusted.

Git can tell you a file changed. eve should tell you what shipped, which behavior changed, how the work was verified, and what future contributors should know before building on it.

## What belongs in product history

An eve snapshot should describe a finished unit of work from the reader's point of view:

- the title and type of change
- the product summary
- the user-visible behavior, when there is one
- decisions that explain why the result looks this way
- risks, tradeoffs, or follow-up concerns
- validation commands and outcomes
- artifacts such as screenshots, videos, logs, URLs, and notes
- relationships to earlier snapshots

A good snapshot is useful months later to someone who was not present when the work happened.

## What does not belong

eve is not a replacement for commit messages, code review comments, issue trackers, or long-form design docs.

Avoid using snapshots for:

- Every intermediate commit
- Raw terminal dumps without interpretation
- Private credentials or secrets
- Unverified claims
- Exhaustive implementation narration that belongs in code review

Keep the snapshot focused on the product result and the evidence for it.

## What good looks like

A weak snapshot says "fixed auth bug."

A useful snapshot says "users are redirected back to the dashboard after GitHub OAuth; verified with `go test ./...` and a login-flow screenshot; risk remains around expired provider sessions."

The second version gives a future reader enough context to understand the shipped behavior and decide what to check before changing it.

## How agents benefit

Agents are good at reading structured local context. eve gives them a stable source of truth that is closer to product intent than a commit log.

Before changing a feature, an agent can inspect related snapshots to understand prior decisions, risks, and validation expectations. After completing work, the agent can write a new snapshot that future agents and humans can trust.
